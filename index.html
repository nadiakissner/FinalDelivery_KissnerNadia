Why pepas are special

Pepas are more than just cookies - they're a cultural icon that brings families together. Whether homebaked with traditional recipes or bought at the supermarket, they are the heart Argentine pastry when it comes to cookies. In their classic form, — soft and buttery vanilla dough and a center of quince jam — they're among the best companions for sharing mate with friends and family.

Eating pepas can be a whole experience. The ritual begins by eating around the jam center, carefully leaving the sweet heart for the end. "Save the best for last," as they say — and that's what we're here for. Once the plain dough is gone, you're left with the jewel: the rich quince center and the soft cookie base beneath it. One or two bites of pure joy. Sure, you could eat it like any other cookie, but our way adds suspense, builds anticipation, and makes the final bite something to savor.

Sweet Fact

Did you know? Pepas were first created in Argentina in the early 20th century as an attempt to recreate those brought by European immigrants. They were made with more delicate fruits, but in Argentina the recipe found its inseparable parter: quince; given its abundance in the region. Quickly became a staple in every household, and, over the years, pepas expanded from artisanal bakeries to big industrial brands of massive consumption. The word "Pepa" is an affectionate nickname for a girl in the Spanish language, capturing the humble, warm and homey nature of this cookie recipe.
